The grid & market structure
I. MARKET STRUCTURE

01
How GB power is sold
Britain runs a single national wholesale marketBETTA — British Electricity Trading and Transmission Arrangements: all GB power trades in one market, not regional zones., priced in half-hourly slots. NESONational Energy System Operator — runs the grid in real time and designs the connection queue. operates the system minute to minute; OfgemOffice of Gas and Electricity Markets — Britain's independent energy regulator. regulates it. There are no regional price zones — one national price, set increasingly by wind.
02
How asset owners' revenues are structured
Three routes:
— A CfDContract for Difference — a 15-year, inflation-linked contract that fixes a generator's price and removes market risk. A fixed price for 15 years (lowest risk).
— MerchantSelling at the open wholesale price with no fixed-price contract — higher risk, higher potential reward. The open market price (highest risk).
— Cap & FloorAn Ofgem scheme for interconnectors and long-duration storage. A regulated floor with capped upside (utility-like).
03
Why connections are the bottleneck
Most cheap generation is stuck in Scotland; most demand is in the South; the wires between can't carry it all. The result is constraintWhen the wires can't carry all the power generated, the grid pays generators (mostly Scottish wind) to switch off — at a cost of ~£2.4bn a year. — and a years-long queue for a firm grid connection. Who holds a connection, and where, is the whole game.

The grid bottleneck — the primary barrier
VII. GRID CONNECTIONS

Everything in the technology section above runs into the same wall: a project cannot earn a penny until it is connected, and the queue for a firm connection is the binding constraint on the entire 2030 build-out. This section explains where the bottleneck is, how the reformed queue works, where capacity is scarce, and when certainty arrives.

Where the bottleneck is — the geography
VII-A. THE CONSTRAINT MAP

The UK grid is, at heart, a geography problem. Cheap wind generation sits in the north (Scotland); demand sits in the south (London, the Midlands, data centres). The transmission boundary between them — the B6 boundary — cannot carry the surplus, so northern wind is paid to switch off.

The gated pathway to the network
VII-B. THE GATED PATHWAY

TMO4+ is a two-gate process. A project first earns a Gate 1 indicative offer, then must satisfy both a Readiness test and a Strategic Alignment test to reach Gate 2 — a firm offer carrying a confirmed date, connection point and queue position. Gate 2 projects are then split into Phase 1 (2030 delivery) and Phase 2 (2035). This module covers transmission and large embedded connections only.

STEP 01
Application
Project applies in a connections window. ~3,000 applications were assessed in the queue-reordering exercise (G2tWQ).
STEP 02
Gate 1
Indicative offer + queue position based on a coordinated network design. No confirmed connection date.
STEP 03
Gate 2 criteria
Must pass both: Readiness (land + planning) and Strategic Alignment (needed for the plan).
STEP 04
Gate 2 offer
Firm offer: confirmed date, connection point and queue position. The bankability event.
STEP 05
Milestones
Queue Management Milestones. M1 = initiate statutory consents & submit planning. Miss them and you can be removed.
STEP 06
Energise
Build and connect. Phase 1 targets 2030 delivery; Phase 2 targets 2035.

Investment playbook
VIII. THE TRADE — BY RISK / RETURN

One ranked set of strategic plays, ordered from regulated yield to opportunistic merchant. Each is tagged with the section above that evidences it.

Core yieldLowest risk · regulated returns
Cap & Floor assets — interconnectors & LDES
Ofgem Cap & Floor backed scheme for interconnectors and long-duration storage is a capital intensive investment with a regulated revenue floor and capped excessive profits with near-utility risk. Evidence: IV (Interconnectors, LDES) · VII-C overhang
Own the Gate 2 position
A confirmed Gate 2 offer fixes date, point and queue position — the single largest de-risking event in the lifecycle. Target Phase 1 (2030) transmission assets receiving offers between mid-May and mid-September 2026, and price the offer itself as an asset. Evidence: VII-B · VII-D timeline
Value-addModerate risk · development upside
Readiness arbitrage — acquire & advance Gate 1
Readiness now turns on land rights and planning, not capital. Acquire Gate 1 projects with secured land/consent and push them through the readiness tests to Gate 2; buy distressed positions from holders unwilling to post security. Consolidation is policy-engineered. Evidence: VII-B · VII-D
CfD + PPA layering
Acquire AR6/AR7-backed assets at strike, then layer a corporate PPA onto the post-CfD tail for the lowest-risk route to UK offtake at scale. Evidence: II (AR7) · IV (Solar, Offshore)
Co-location, not standalone
Solar + BESS and wind + BESS on Gate 2 sites with existing import/export capacity. Avoid standalone merchant solar — capture-rate compression is structural, not cyclical. Evidence: III (Issues) · IV (Solar, BESS)
Opportunistic / merchantHighest risk · market-priced
BESS cycling against constraint payments
Where battery connection capacity is abundant (~153 GW unprioritised), the moat is not the connection but the trade: cycle against price spreads and constraint payments, with optimiser quality and locational basis driving returns. Evidence: IV (BESS) · VII-C
Northern constraint trades
Long Scottish BESS priced for curtailment relief; short merchant Scottish wind until the ASTI transmission build closes the boundary gap post-2028. Floating offshore options in the Celtic Sea carry scarcity premia. Evidence: VII-A map · II (curtailment)

Counter-view: NESO's own timeline has already slipped, locational sequencing creates winners and losers by region, and the ~217 GW of projects that received Gate 1 only may re-enter via future windows — so a Gate 2 position confers timing certainty, not exclusivity. Not investment advice.
